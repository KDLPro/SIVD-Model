Introduction

In December 2019, the first case of COVID-19 was detected in Wuhan, China. Back then, the disease was known as the 2019 novel coronavirus (2019-nCoV). The disease quickly spread worldwide, eventually resulting in the COVID-19 pandemic.

This disease was caused by the severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2), which can spread via droplets/aerosols and small airborne particles. Infected people spread those particles each time they talk, breathe, cough, sneeze, or sing. Poorly-ventilated areas allows for SARS-CoV-2 to spread more easily. Various researches have studied the spread of COVID-19 via various agent-based models to simulate the pandemic. These studies allow governments to make educated plans and strategies to control the spread of the pandemic.

Agent-Based Modeling is a powerful tool for simulating real-world scenarios, thus it is an important tool for studying the speed of infection of diseases such as COVID-19, as well as the effects of control measures to reduce the speed of the infection. Researching the spread of a disease is important to minimize the impacts of future outbreaks and/or pandemics.

The goal of this simulation is to use ABM to simulate the spread of COVID-19 and to compare the effects of interventions done by governments around the world to mitigate the spread of COVID-19. 

What is Agent-Based Modeling?

Agent-Based Modeling is a bottom-up simulation where a system is analyzed by its individual agents that interact with each other.

For example, if we want to predict the number of cases of Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS-CoV) in a particular region, we can create agents that represent the people in the region, where some are wearing masks while some doesnt, some travel a lot, and some dont. We then simulate how these agents interact with each other and the associated probabilities of MERS-CoV being transmitted.


Figure 1.1 Agent-Based Modeling Visualization

After running the simulation, we can understand the system: the number of infections, how many have recovered or died from the disease, transmission rates, among others. We can also analyze how the system would look like if the government implements interventions such as quarantine and border control.

An ability to understand macro phenomena emerging from microscale behavior is a cornerstone of ABM. ABM emphasizes the behavior of individual agents, which allows it to be more flexible. A granular bottom-up approach allows the modeling system to capture some aspects that a macro-scale model cannot. (Karami, 2021)

What is NetLogo?

NetLogo is a free, programmable modeling environment that can simulate complex simulations such as natural and social events. It is also a platform that allows for simulating Agent-Based Modeling. It has a graphical user interface (GUI) for designing as well as simulating ABMs, allowing it to be accessible for teachers and students, even with minimal programming experience. It is also powerful enough to serve as a tool for researchers in many fields.

About the Model

Theoretical Basis and Constants Used in the Model

Figure 2.1 The SIVD Model

The theoretical basis for the model used in the case study is the SIR model, which represents the interactions between susceptible (S), infected (I), and removed (R) individuals in a closed population over time. But this SIR model has been modified since this blog was written in 2023, where vaccines for COVID-19 have been released. With this development, the SIR model is modified to account for vaccinated individuals. This model is called Susceptible-Infected-Vaccinated-Dead (SIVD) model, as shown in Figure 2.1

Here are the four classes of people in the study:

  1. Susceptible (S): The individuals of that can be infected by COVID-19. They may be infected or immune to the disease if they get the vaccine.
  2. Infected (I): The individuals that are infected by COVID-19. They may be cured and become susceptible again or die from the disease.
  3. Vaccinated (V): The individuals who got the vaccine for COVID-19 and become immune to the disease.
  4. Dead (D): The individuals who died due to the disease.
The rates related to the disease are the following:
  1. Infectivity (): The chance that the coronavirus infects a new person.
  2. Vaccination Chance (): The chance that a susceptible person decides to get a vaccine.
  3. Recovery Chance (): The chance that an infected person recovers from COVID-19.
  4. Lethality (): The chance that an infected person would die due to COVID-19.
The differential equations that define the SIVD model are shown below:
Figure 2.2 Differential Equations for the SIVD Model

The model has a following assumptions:
  • There are no births in this population.
  • The population moves at random, so some people may frequently contact other people while some people rarely do.
  • The transmission of coronavirus only occurs between humans.
  • Recovered individuals are still susceptible for infection unless they receive the vaccine after recovering from the disease.
  • Vaccination doesn't start immediately. When the vaccination period starts, susceptible people may get vaccinated.

NetLogo Embedded Simulation and Explanation of Test Settings

In this blog, we will simulate the SIVD model using agent-based modeling in NetLogo. From here, we can set the initial parameters of the simulation:
  1. Initial Population: How many people in the population initially. This value ranges from 50 to 300 and can be increased in increments of 1.
  2. Initially Infected: How many people get infected at the start of the simulation. This value ranges from 1 to 40 and can be increased in increments of 1
From there, we can modify the parameters of COVID-19:
  1. Infectivity (): This value ranges from 0% to 99.9% and can be increased in increments of 0.1%. 
  2. Infection Radius: Susceptible people within the infection radius may get infected. Its value ranges from 1 to 5 spaces and can be increased in increments of 0.5 spaces.
  3. Lethality (): Its value ranges from 0% to 99.9% and can be increased in increments of 0.1%.
  4. Death Onset: After this period, the infected person has a chance to die every single day, depending on the diseases lethality. Its value ranges from 1 week to 40 weeks and can be increased in increments of 1 week.
  5. Recovery Chance (): This value ranges from 0% to 99.9% and can be increased in increments of 0.1%.
  6. Start of Recovery: After this period, the infected person has a chance to recover every single day, depending on the diseases recovery chance. Its value ranges from 1 week to 40 weeks and can be increased in increments of 1 day.
  7. Vaccination Chance (): Its value ranges from 0% to 99.9% and can be increased in increments of 0.1%.
  8. Start of Vaccination: Sets the start of the vaccination period, where susceptible people get vaccinated and therefore become immune to the disease. Its value ranges from 1 to 200 weeks and can be increased in increments of 1 week.
The default values for infectivity (7%), lethality (7%), and recovery chance (12%) were taken from the study "Modified SIR model for COVID-19 transmission dynamics: Simulation with case study of UK, US and India", specifically from the data gathered during April - May 2020 in the US. Since not all values are found in the study, they are set arbitrarily. All the values can be modified to see how the infection plays out when these values are adjusted.


This simulation uses the following values: 
  • Initial Population: 300 people
  • Initially Infected: 40 people
  • Infectivity: 7%
  • Infection Radius: 3 spaces
  • Lethality: 7%
  • Death Onset: 4 weeks
  • Recovery Chance: 10%
  • Start of Recovery: 7 days
  • Vaccination Chance: 9%
  • Start of Vaccination: 52 weeks
From the start of the simulation, we can see that the number of infected people started to go up at first. However, as people die due to the disease, the number of infected people slowly decreases while the number of susceptible people stays at a steady level. Eventually, the gap between the number of infected people and the number of susceptible people closes. Then, when the vaccination period starts, infected people started getting their vaccines, sharply raising the number of vaccinated people as the number of infected and susceptible people sharply declines, until the infection dies out.

In Simulation 4, the lethality is 14%, double that of the default value seen in Simulation 1. On the other hand, the death onset is reduced to 3 weeks from the default value of 4 weeks. In both Simulations 4 and 5, we can see that the number of deaths due to the disease are higher than in Simulation 1, the shorter death onset have a significant impact on the increase of the number of deaths in the simulation. This is because the shorter death onset reduces the chances of the patient being cured. A longer death onset means that the patient have more chances to get cured before succumbing to the disease.

Simulation 6: Higher Recovery Chance and Simulation 7: Shorter Start of Recovery

Simulation 7

In simulation 6, where the recovery chance is doubled, the number of infected and susceptible people maintains at around 200 persons and 100 persons. There are fluctuations in the number of infected people, but there are no spikes in the infected population as they have higher chances to get cured. And since the recovery chance is increased, the number of people who died due to the disease decreased significantly.

On the other hand, in Simulation 7, the start of recovery becomes 4 days instead of the default 7 days. The number of infected people steadily declines but there deaths didn't get reduced significantly. However, there are more people who survived the disease than that of Simulation 1.

Simulation 8: Earlier Start of Vaccination and Simulation 9: Late Start of Vaccination

Simulation 8

Simulation 9

In Simulation 8, the vaccination period starts after 40 weeks, 12 weeks earlier than the default value. Simulation 9 moved the vaccination period to 104 weeks after the start of the simulation. The early vaccination period in Simulation 8 helped in reducing the number of deaths attributed to the disease. On the other hand, the longer vaccination period means more time for the disease to kill the population. That's why scientists tried their best to release the COVID-19 vaccine early. It's also the reason why governments approved the experimental vaccine to reduce the number of deaths due to the disease.

Simulation 10: Higher Vaccination Rate

Simulation 10

In this simulation, the vaccination rate is 18%, which is double that of the default value. With this increase, the pandemic ends earlier than normal, at 59 weeks, compared to the 63.3 weeks result in Simulation 1.

Overall, here are takeaways of this simulation:
  1. When the vaccination period starts, the number of infected persons start to decline until there are no more infected people left due to the lack of susceptible people to infect.
  2. If the death onset is long enough, then the disease would be less likely to kill people, especially if the recovery period is less than the death onset.
  3. A high infectivity and infection radius makes it easy for the virus to spread through the community.
